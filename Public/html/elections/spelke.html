Sakai Board Nominee

Kenneth Spelke
University of Illinois


Bio:

Ken Spelke is Associate Dean for Information Technology and Research at the Graduate School of Library and Information Science, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign (www.lis.uiuc.edu/oitr). Prior to his current position, Ken served for six years as Assistant Dean and Director of Information Technology and Communication Services at the College of Agricultural, Consumer and Environmental Sciences, University of Illinois. In that role, Ken was Chief Information Officer for the College of Agriculture and for the University of Illinois Extension.

An information technology manager with over 30 years experience in higher education, Spelke has served as Supervisor of Television Production at Columbia University (New York City); Director of Media Services for the Department of Psychiatry and Behavioral Science, State University of New York at Stony Brook; and Director of the Center for Instructional Media Support, Queens College of the City University of New York. Prior to coming to the University of Illinois in 1998, Spelke was Acting Assistant Vice President for Information Technology at Queens College where he served as the campus’ first CIO, developing the Office of Information Technology (OIT), a merger of Academic Computing, Administrative Computing, and Media Technology Services.

Ken continues to be an active member of ACE, the Association for Communications Excellence (www.aceweb.org), where he most recently served as 2004-2005 Chair of the Leadership and Management SIG. He currently serves on the Board of Directors of the American Distance Education Consortium, ADEC (www.adec.edu), and he is a member of the Advanced Engineering Taskforce of the Illinois Century Network, a state agency funded at $27M annually with responsibility for providing Internet access to public schools, libraries, health facilities, and other agencies and organizations throughout Illinois. Spelke is active in several Educause committees, and he is the University of Illinois’ Sakai SEPP Representative.

Ken received his bachelor’s degree in Communications and Journalism from Fordham University, a Masters Degree in Public Administration and Political Science from the City University of New York, and he is currently completing his Ph.D. in Library and Information Science at the University of Illinois. His research interests include information literacy, use of the Internet as a research tool, digital divide access issues, and the evolution and adoption of eXtension.

Ken and his wife Emily live in Champaign, Illinois, along with their three year old lab, July. Their twin daughters, Dawn and Pam, began college careers of their own, as freshmen, this past September.

Vision Statement on Learning and Collaborative Tools

Ken Spelke, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign

I feel strongly that all tools being developed should meet the pedagogical soundness test: “What teaching / learning challenges will a particular proposed tool help solve?” I believe we should never develop a product or a process just because “it’s cool,” but because it has real potential to advance education in a specific and tangible way – and equally important – that it will be superior in fit and function to commercial or other open source tools already available.

I believe in a rich mix of collaborative tools from many sources. There are good commercial products currently available which have good features and perform reliably – I think we have enough to do without reinventing wheels. I would like to see faculty, students, and administrators able to comfortably and knowledgeably choose from a wide variety of open source and commercial products so that each course or project benefits from access to the “best of the best.” I do not adhere to the philosophy of “it’s only good if it’s invented here.” That said, I think a major challenge for the Sakai Foundation will be to ensure interoperability of Sakai tools with other open source and commercial products, across all major computing platforms, and with the major institutional administrative enterprise systems like Banner, PeopleSoft, and others.

Another area that I am passionate about is a commitment to ensuring that the tools our community develops are accessible and functional for disabled users. This is a digital divide issue that the educational community must actively and relentlessly seek to overcome.

In terms of leadership style, I always try to be as inclusive as possible, since good decision-making requires thorough, accurate, and diverse input. Consensus building is important in any organization, but I think particularly so in a community like Sakai. Often, we face not the question of “what is best,” but what is appropriate within a particular context, what is possible to achieve within limitations of time and available resources, and which investments in time and energy have the greatest potential to benefit significant numbers of Sakai stakeholders. I also believe, to borrow from the terminology of social network analysis, in the great value of brokers and bridges – individuals who try to see common areas of interest and effort, identify potentially efficacious partnerships, and then do all possible to create environments which foster and support them. In all of the professional organizations I actively participate in, I have always taken great pleasure in identifying partnership potentials and working to facilitate multi-state, multi-institutional initiatives.

Finally, as a parent of two new college freshmen, I feel a direct and deep personal interest in doing all possible to help create an educational environment which truly encourages and enhances learning, and provides the infrastructure for developing 21st century technological competencies for our instructors and our students. This is about empowerment…it is essential for ensuring the lifelong and just-in-time learning skills which will undoubtedly create opportunities for advancement and improved quality of life for people in all societies and across all nations.
