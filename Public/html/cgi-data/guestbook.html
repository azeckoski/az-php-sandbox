    SUMMARY
    There is no more important investment that a country can make than in the education of its citizens. For equitable access to positive economic development and social progress, and to full enjoyment of life, learning is essential. Yet worldwide over a hundred million children grow up without entering a schoolhouse door, and many millions more do not complete primary school or go to very weak schools. What is needed is a `good' marriage between available technology and the educational process that enhances learning, while maintaining equity and quality.

    We need major improvements in learning, both in the developing world and in countries such as the United States. Many adults are functionally illiterate in the United States. Every politician announces that she or he will improve learning, but it is not happening. Our present strategies, even with improvements now being implemented and contemplated, will not suffice. If anything, the evidence might indicate that learning is declining everywhere.

    This proposal explores a strategy that may lead to a practical order of magnitude improvement in learning. The model is tutorial learning, such as that seen with Socrates. In the suggested materials the computer will be the tutor. No new hardware or software is required.

    We need a form of distance learning that

    1) Focuses on student learning problems

    2) Works for very large numbers of students of widely different backgrounds, globally

    3) Is highly adaptive to the needs of each student, interactive, and tutorial

    4) Works in schools and also where no schools or universities exist

    5) Is affordable and available for everyone on earth

    6) Is sustainable for both the rich and the poor

    This proposal suggests a way to achieve these goals.

    ACHIEVING EDUCATION FOR ALL

    Why are we not realizing these universally desired goals? A major problem is that there are too many people on earth for the current educational systems. Schools and universities developed when we had far fewer people. What has been lost as numbers increased is the ability to individualize education, to personalize it to the needs and problems of each student. Technology can offer immense help in learning, allowing us to attain education for all, but not the way technology is used in education today.
    We first consider the overall feature of the learning units, then the material to be developed in an initial experiment, the reasons why such an extensive experiment is necessary, and questions to be answered by the experiment. Finally we discuss the steps after the experiment leading to global education.

    Features of this approach to learning

    • Adaptive learning for all students. Each student learns at a unique pace, and continues in a given area until success is obtained. Each student learns to her or his full capacity.

    The more we learn, the more we recognize the unique complexity of any individual intellect and the stronger the conclusion becomes that the individuality inherent in our brain networks makes that of fingerprints or facial features gross and simple by comparison.

    Roger Sperry, Nobel Prize Speech, December 8, 1981.

    • Highly interactive computer based learning modules. The system for producing such modules, described later, has been under development for many years. The designers are excellent teachers in the area involved.

    • Tutorial modules, with frequent questions from the computer. The time between significant student inputs is typically less than 20 seconds.


    • Student answers are free form. Multiple choice is not used. Students can say anything, using their natural languages.

    • Questions and replies determine student learning problems. Help is given. The program verifies that the help was successful. The program decides based on student input and past performance what learning material is to be presented next. All this is determined by the designers.

    • Storage and use of student information assists with student adaptation.

    • The units are developed in many environments

    • The units are evaluated in many environments

    &amp#61656; Evaluation groups in the five countries and languages occur in the initial experiment suggested. Likely possibilities are Chinese, English, Spanish, an Indian language, and an African language. As units for new cultures are developed, each is evaluated.

    &amp#61656; Evaluated with thousands of all types of students, including wide economic backgrounds, gender, and cultural backgrounds.

    &amp#61656; Evaluated both in schools and in locations with no or weak schools

    • Voice input and output are used. Natural language recognition, in the artificial intelligence sense, is not needed.

    • Peer learning with small groups of students at the displays encourages critical thinking, increases student interests, and increases retention.

    • If teachers and other adults are available, they can play an important role.

    • Current technology is sufficient. No tactics from artificial intelligence are needed.

    This experiment will provide the necessary information.

    Cost of the experiment

    Cost for learning in the new system

    For sustainability a critical factor is the cost for a student hour of learning. This must include all factors, including development, distribution, maintenance, organizations, facilities, teacher training (if any), publicity, and profit. Too often only some of these factors are considered. For schools in the United States the current cost is about $10.00 for a student hour of learning. We expect the new system to cost less than existing modes of learning when it is used widely.
    An analysis for the costs of adaptive tutorial learning is contained in Chapter 12 in Tutorial Distance Learning. Costs go down as more people use the system, desirable for global learning. Some cost factors depend on further information, but the new system should be less expensive than existing education. Funds already spent for education in economically deprived parts of the world, either local or from international agencies such as the World Bank, can yield more and better learning with this new method.

Some of these profits will be used for further development, as outlined, and for distribution in economically deprived areas. Thus by providing better education for children in wealthy countries we would help provide better education for the poor of the world. Less expensive learning devices will help.
